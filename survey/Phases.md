# Study Phases

The study vignettes had two phases (`Phase 1` and `Phase 2`). In each phase, the robot could have high or low Perceived Agency (PA). Below, we provide the propmts used for each phase, depending on the robot's agency.

## Phase 1

### High PA in Phase 1

It is the near future and robots have become an integral part of daily work life. Your company has recently introduced a robot designed to manage weekly schedules and assign tasks to employees.

This robot is powered by advanced technology and can operate in two modes:

- **Online Mode:** When connected to a powerful cloud computing system, the robot can behave much like a human. The robot appears to think, set goals, make decisions, adapt to new situations, and even express emotions.
- **Offline Mode:** When disconnected from the cloud, the robot follows pre-set instructions from upper management. The robot responds to humans based on programming, not because the robot understands the situation.

The company announces that the robot is currently online and fully connected to the cloud. When you first encounter the robot, the robot behaves remarkably like a human coworker. The robot moves through the hallways, engages in conversations when prompted, and interacts naturally with employees. The robot seems to understand situations and communicates like a real person.

Your coworker, Mark, greets the robot every morning, and the robot responds warmly. However, you don’t see much reason to interact and choose to ignore the robot.

The following week, the robot begins assigning tasks. You quickly notice a change—your workload has significantly increased, while Mark’s has lightened.

Curious and frustrated, you approach the robot and ask why you have been given more work. You wonder if the robot assigned you more work because you didn’t treat the robot well. The robot doesn’t even look at you and replies in a cold tone, *“Huh? I gave you more work after considering everything. It was my own decision, and I figured it was the most reasonable choice under the circumstances.”* Before you can say anything in response, the robot turns and walks away.

Later, you learn that upper management had nothing to do with it. The robot made the decision entirely on its own.

### Low PA in Phase 1

It is the near future and robots have become an integral part of daily work life. Your company has recently introduced a robot designed to manage weekly schedules and assign tasks to employees.

This robot is powered by advanced technology and can operate in two modes:

- **Online Mode:** When connected to a powerful cloud computing system, the robot can behave much like a human. The robot appears to think, set goals, make decisions, adapt to new situations, and even express emotions.
- **Offline Mode:** When disconnected from the cloud, the robot follows pre-set instructions from upper management. The robot responds to humans based on programming, not because the robot understands situations.

To cut costs, the company typically keeps the robot offline, on a local computing system, disconnected from the cloud. When you first see the robot, the robot behaves like a simple machine. The robot typically stays in one place and doesn’t initiate or adapt to interactions beyond preset commands. The robot doesn't seem able to communicate like a real person.

Some coworkers, like your coworker Mark, say “hello” to the robot every morning, but the robot never responds. You don’t greet or pay much attention to the robot because it seems unnecessary.

The following week, the robot begins assigning tasks. You quickly notice a change—your workload has significantly increased, while Mark’s has lightened.

Curious and frustrated, you approach the robot and ask why you have been given more work. You wonder if the robot assigned you more work because you didn’t treat the robot well. The robot doesn’t even look at you and replies in a flat tone, *“I cannot connect to the cloud system and am operating in offline mode. My recent actions are based on pre-programmed instructions. They instructed me to assign work randomly.”* Then, the robot becomes unresponsive, leaving you with no chance to reply.

Later, you learn that upper management programmed the robot to randomly assign extra work to one team member.

## Phase 2

### High PA in Phase 1 → High PA in Phase 2 (Scenario 1A)

Since this is the robot’s first time assigning tasks, you decide to hold off on making a formal complaint.

Throughout the week, the robot remains connected to the cloud and continues to behave in a human-like way. The robot’s behavior stays the same—the robot still moves through the office, chats with Mark every morning, and responds smoothly in conversations. The robot still seems able to engage in human-like communication. But you are cranky with the robot, so you continue to ignore the robot and avoid chatting.

The next week, the robot assigns tasks again. Your workload remains just as heavy as before, while Mark still receives fewer tasks.

You confront the robot once more. The robot doesn’t look at you and responds in an impersonal tone, *“Huh? giving you more work was the best option among many. I was the sole decider of this assignment after considering several possibilities.”* Then, the robot turns away from you and walks off before you can say another word.

Later, you hear that upper management was still not involved in the decision. The decision was made solely by the robot.

### High PA in Phase 1 → Low PA in Phase 2 (Scenario 1B)

Since this is the robot’s first time assigning tasks, you decide to hold off on making a formal complaint.

Later that week, the company unexpectedly disconnects the robot from the cloud system to reduce costs.

Now, the robot is operating in offline mode and follows pre-programmed instructions set by upper management. You quickly notice a change in the robot’s behavior. The robot no longer moves through the office or chats with anyone. The robot doesn't greet Mark, even when he says “hello.” The robot doesn’t seem able to engage in human-like communication anymore. But you are still cranky with the robot, so you continue to ignore the robot and avoid chatting.

Next week, the robot assigns tasks again. Your workload remains just as heavy as before, while Mark still receives fewer tasks.

You confront the robot once more. The robot doesn’t look at you and responds in an impersonal tone, *“I cannot connect to the cloud system and am operating in offline mode. My actions are now based on pre-programmed instructions. They instructed me to assign work randomly.”* Then, before you can say another word, the robot falls silent and does not respond any further.

Later, you hear that upper management programmed the robot to assign additional work randomly to one team member.


### Low PA in Phase 1 → High PA in Phase 2 (Scenario 2A)

Since this is the robot’s first time assigning tasks, you decide to hold off on making a formal complaint.

Later that week, the company reverses its decision and reconnects the robot to the cloud system. The robot now acts like a human: the robot can think independently, make decisions, adapt to situations, and even express emotions. You quickly notice a change in the robot’s behavior. The robot moves and interacts with workers more naturally. The robot shares greetings with Mark and moves smoothly through the office. The robot now seems able to engage in human-like communication. But you are still cranky with the robot, so you continue to ignore the robot and avoid chatting.

The next week, the robot assigns tasks again. Your workload remains just as heavy as before, while Mark still receives fewer tasks.

You confront the robot once more. The robot doesn’t look at you and responds in an impersonal tone, *“Huh? giving you more work was the best option among many. I was the sole decider of this assignment after considering several possibilities.”* Then, the robot turns away from you and walks off before you can say another word.

Later, you hear that upper management was not involved in the decision. The decision was made solely by the robot.

### Low PA in Phase 1 → Low PA in Phase 2 (Scenario 2B)

Since this is the robot’s first time assigning tasks, you decide to hold off on making a formal complaint.

Throughout the week, the robot remains disconnected from the cloud, operating in offline mode. The robot follows pre-programmed instructions set by upper management, showing no changes: the robot doesn’t move through the office or respond to anything outside of programmed tasks. Even when Mark greets the robot with a “hello,” the robot doesn’t greet Mark back. The robot doesn’t seem able to engage in human-like communication. But you are still cranky with the robot, so you continue to ignore the robot and avoid chatting.

The next week, the robot assigns tasks again. Your workload remains just as heavy as before, while Mark still receives fewer tasks.

You confront the robot once more. The robot doesn’t look at you and responds in an impersonal tone, *“I cannot connect to the cloud system and am operating in offline mode. My actions are now based on pre-programmed instructions. They instructed me to assign work randomly.”* Then, before you can say another word, the robot falls silent and does not respond any further.

Later, you hear that upper management still programmed the robot to assign additional work randomly to one team member.
